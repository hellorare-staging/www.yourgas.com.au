Five things to watch for at the park this spring

After the longest Perth winter in 30 years, everyone is thrilled to see the sun come out and the weather warm up.

And with so many fantastic green spaces in WA, why not get out in the park and let the kids have a fun in the sun. However, while the local park might not seem a risky place to visit, keeping your little ones safe is still important. ATCO Gas Australia has compiled a list of the most common hazards to watch out for in parks this spring to ensure you and your family have a safe and fun time outdoors.

  1. Playground equipment

    The worst assumption we can make is thinking that all parks are set up for our safety. Unfortunately, this isn’t always the case. Lots of the monkey bars and swings in playgrounds are made from metal, and in our hot Perth sunshine will burn our hands. Not a surprise that you want your children to experience.

    Older suburbs may also have parks with broken or missing rails, wobbly slides or broken ropes. Give it a quick check over before your children get started and make sure you look for spiders lurking in the crevices.

  2. Traffic and bike safety

    Obviously traffic isn’t a problem within the park, but many of our parks border onto busy main roads. Be aware when entering and exiting the park, especially if you have bicycles, skateboards or roller-blades that make it harder to stop. Remind little ones that once they get to the park they can run free, but to be alert to road rules outside of the wide, open, green spaces.

    Also, if you are not on foot, be sure to wear a helmet, particularly when you’re heading to the skate park!

  3. Over-friendly (or less than friendly) pets

    We all love dogs, but that doesn’t always mean they love us! Be cautious of furry friends that are off leads in the park. Quick movements from kids can switch on a dog’s prey drive. If it isn’t your dog it’s best to avoid patting, unless a dog owner you trust tells you it is ok to do so.

  4. Deceptively deep ponds and lakes

    A hazard that we don’t often consider at the park is water. Drowning can occur even in very shallow water. If there isn’t much of a bank between the grass and body of water it can be all too easy to fall in. Keep little ones close at all times and encourage them to avoid going beyond the grass or sand

  5. A gas leak

    It’s uncommon, but if you’re in a park and catch the smell of rotten eggs, it could be a gas leak. If you identify a gas leak, leave the area immediately and don’t use anything that might create a spark, such as a mobile phone, electrical switch or matches. Then call ATCO Gas on 13 13 52. For more information about gas safety outdoors, visit www.yourgas.com.au

Five things you should know before your Spring garden blitz.

Never has Perth been so happy to see the sun out and feel the warmth on our skin after the coldest, wettest winter in decades.

With the sun finally out, it’s time to give your garden the spruce up it needs to get it flourishing and ready for summer entertaining.

A great garden not only adds value to your house, but studies show that gardening both lowers blood pressure and improves your love life.

However, with the benefits of gardening, come some risks. Perth’s gas network operator, ATCO Gas Australia has compiled a list of the most common garden hazards to avoid this Spring.

  1. Bugs

    Speak to any tourist and their number one concern is the dangerous Australian wildlife. We wouldn’t go that far, but bites, stings and irritations are never fun, especially when these bugs carry diseases or are poisonous. Wear a hat, gloves and full-length clothing where possible to avoid exposed skin. Consider tucking your pants into your socks if traipsing through dense garden. If reaching into hidden crevices, such as gas meters and old pot plants, watch out for spiders that might have made the space their home.

  2. Tools of the trade

    Power tools might make the job easier but can cause serious injuries if you’re not dressed properly. Always wear sturdy shoes, long pants and protective eyewear to shield yourself from flying debris and other hazards. Earplugs are a great idea if you’re doing a lot of work with loud power tools. Remember, Australia has the highest rate of skin cancer, so applying sunscreen regularly is also a must.

  3. Harsh chemicals and pesticides

    Use natural alternatives to pesticides and compost where possible. If pesticides are necessary, always read the label to ensure you are using the product correctly. Keep children clear when spraying and always change your clothes before re-entering the home. When in doubt, speak with an expert at your local gardening centre.

  4. Deceptively deep ponds and lakes

    A hazard that we don’t often consider at the park is water. Drowning can occur even in very shallow water. If there isn’t much of a bank between the grass and body of water it can be all too easy to fall in. Keep little ones close at all times and encourage them to avoid going beyond the grass or sand.

  5. Poisonous plants

    If you have children or pets, always find out if flowers or plants are poisonous before purchasing or potting in your garden. Even some of the most beautiful flowers can be poisonous if ingested - we are looking at you poinsettias, hydrangeas, and rhododendrons.

  6. A gas leak

    It’s uncommon, but if you’re working in your garden and catch the smell of rotten eggs, it could be a gas leak. In fact, most gas leaks are reported from the home or garden. If you identify a gas leak, leave the area immediately and don’t use anything that might create a spark, such as a mobile phone, electrical switch or matches. Then call ATCO Gas on 13 13 52.

Get the facts on gas

  • What is natural gas?

    Natural gas consists mainly of methane and is lighter than air, so if it escapes it will rise and disperse into the environment.

  • Why does gas smell?

    The gas we supply to your home does not naturally smell. That’s why we’ve added a distinctive rotten egg odour to your gas supply to allow for easy detection before it reaches unsafe levels.

  • What should I do if I smell gas?

    Fire, explosions and asphyxiation could all result from a gas leak. If you can smell gas it’s important you contact ATCO Gas Australia immediately on 13 13 52.

    We are available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week and will be able to assist you in locating and repairing the leak (local call fee from anywhere in the state excluding mobiles).

To find out more about natural gas, including how to make sure your gas appliances are operating as safely and efficiently as possible, visit the FAQ section on the ATCO Gas Australia website or download our PDF guide.

Additional information, including how to get your property connected to natural gas, can be found by visiting Alinta Energy or Kleenheat Gas.
